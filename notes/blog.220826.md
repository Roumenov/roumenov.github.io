## Tl;dr

If you are asked a question, for fuck's sake, ANSWER IT. Don't ask why they're asking. Don't be cagey trying to guess whether they're on the wrong track. It's not going to work. For every rare occasion where you save them some time, you'll turn several more conversations into a sprawling mess that loses and confuses everyone.

## Non-Organization

We have always generally followed the Valve way of non-organization. Organization is a natural tendency and doesn't need to be forced where there are motivated people. What this means in practice is that the outcomes hinge on our ability to coordinate.

Coordination is one of the hardest things about teamwork and teamwork and one of the main reasons people cite for wanting to be, "their own boss". Your main interface to coordinate is talking. Some kind of discourse happens, and at the conclusion, we all expect to have a vector for our efforts.

>Conversational norms in a group are the wheels that move your pieces into position.

## Question, Question?

A favorite approach in _philosophical_ discourse(which I ♥) is the Socratic Dialogue. Rather than making a statement, you pose a question with the intent of leading toward an investigation of the intended statement. It's a very good way to prompt further discourse and get people to turn ideas over in their head.

However, we're not trying to make conversations bigger and mor expensive. We want small, efficient wheels.

## Raising the Cost of Questions

The nice thing about communication is that it's cheap. Communication is ideal when it's clear and purposeful, but not too precious. The more precious it gets, the more people will find reasons to hesitate. Enough wheel to roll over bumps, not enough to affect our gas mileage.

If you are curious about what they're doing, their goals, their methods, ask. But **do not refuse their question** with your interrogation. The biggest problem with the "Question? Question?" dynamic isn't even the spiraling dialogue and cascading complexity it creates. It's the fact that people constantly adapt to norms. Not only will it get others in the team to feel comfortable with this norm, in the macrocosm of your team dynamic, people will learn that questions lead to long and tedious exchanges. They will find the lowest friction way to mitigate the cost to themselves. This is where people start tuning out in meetings, stop asking questions, and wait for it to end.

> Your coworkers are now hostages.

The ability to disengage is an _existential requirement_ for any kind of [[mutual agreement]]. The moment one or both parties can't disengage of their own choice, it has ceased to be a mutual agreement. If you get into a pattern of making questions expensive, people will seek other ways to get answers. The problem is that work is antithetical to philosophy. If someone is trying to finish a task, the last thing they want to get into is an investigation over said task.

Have a lot of spare wheels, keep 'em greasy, keep 'em replaceable.
